Review

 

In the fourth quarter (January-March) of 2022-23, India's current account deficit (CAD) contracted to $1.3 billion, equivalent to 0.2 percent of the GDP, compared to $16.8 billion or 2 percent of GDP in the previous quarter. The decrease in CAD can be attributed to a reduction in the trade deficit, which declined to $52.6 billion in Q4 of 2022-23 from $71.3 billion in Q3 of 2022-23, as well as strong performance in services exports, as reported by the Reserve Bank of India (RBI).

Merchandise exports experienced a growth of 6.3 percent, aided by a surplus in the invisibles account, which includes services, transfers, and income. The invisibles account improved to $198.2 billion, primarily driven by the services sector. Software services exports reached $146.8 billion. Additionally, private transfers, including remittances from overseas Indians, increased to $112.5 billion in FY23, compared to $89.1 billion in FY22.

The surplus in the capital account, representing the flow of capital, decreased from $85.9 billion in FY22 to $59 billion in FY23. This decline was primarily attributed to reduced inflows of foreign direct investment, which moderated to $28 billion in FY23 from $38.6 billion in FY22. Foreign portfolio investment also experienced outflows of $5.2 billion, although it was lower compared to the outflows of $16.8 billion in the same period the previous year. Additionally, even external commercial borrowing (ECB) inflows were lower due to higher global interest rates, resulting in net outflows of $3.8 billion in ECBs.

Going forward, it is expected that CAD will moderate.
